BigOps: an operations command center for three countries

Problem

Operational truth lived in five places at once: a legacy order system, Airtable, spreadsheets, inboxes and people's heads. Hundreds of on-hold orders, up to 15 live release campaigns and 100+ freelancers, with no single view of any of it.

What I built

One platform where the team sees everything: which release campaigns are on track, which orders are stuck and why, what stock is running low, who is working where across the three branches, and what the company is spending. AI reads each stuck order and suggests the reason, so triage starts from an answer instead of a blank. Behind the scenes it pulls from eleven different systems automatically, around the clock, so nobody copies numbers between spreadsheets. I designed and built all of it myself.

Impact

Around 23 hours of manual reporting automated every week. On-hold triage went from minutes per order to seconds. Fifteen to twenty staff across three countries now work from one screen.

CS/02
ForecastingInventoryCost savings

Frame stock prediction: forecasting that pays for itself

Problem

Frames were bought reactively. Stockouts stalled framing lines mid-project; overstock tied up cash across three warehouses. Nobody could say what next quarter's demand looked like per frame type, per branch.

What I built

A system that predicts how many frames each branch will need over the next three months. It looks at what sold in the same months last year, adjusts for how the business is trending, adds a safety margin, then subtracts what is already on the shelf and what is already on order. Out comes a simple answer: exactly what to buy, per frame type, per branch. A second, independent check recalculates everything from scratch so the numbers can be trusted.

Impact

$30k to $40k saved per year. Stockouts and overstock down 10 to 25%. Reordering moved from gut feel to a defensible number.

CS/03
Production planning$200k projectDelivered on time

Magnum Photos: production control for a $200k deadline

Problem

One of the company's most complex projects: roughly 5,000 bespoke-framed prints across nine production streams in three countries, staggered hard deadlines, artwork holds that blocked chunks of the queue, and zero slack in the schedule.

What I built

A production control system that told every team exactly how much to produce each day to hit the deadline: take what is left, divide by the working days remaining, adjust automatically as the numbers move. Charts showed at a glance whether each production stream was ahead or behind. When artwork was held up, those orders were set aside without stalling the rest. The whole thing rebuilt itself overnight from the latest order data, with about 156,000 live calculations doing the arithmetic so the team never had to.

Impact

Delivered in two months, on timeline. Slippage was visible the day it happened, not the week after, and the plan reallocated work the moment any production line fell behind.

CS/04
UI / UX designPrototype to production

On-hold report: redesigning triage as a visual hierarchy

Problem

Hundreds of on-hold orders rendered as one flat list. No hierarchy, no state language, no way to see at a glance which client needed action today and which had been waiting silently for a week.

What I built

I redesigned the screen from scratch. Orders group under each client, the reason they are stuck sits beneath, and colour-coded labels say exactly where things stand: needs action, awaiting reply, unresponsive, resolved. Anything waiting too long gets flagged automatically at the top. I built a working prototype first, then shipped it: the finished product went live almost exactly as designed.

Impact

Triage now follows the visual hierarchy instead of a scroll-and-hope search. The pattern became the interaction template for the rest of the platform.

CS/05
AI toolsInstant notificationsHands-free workflows

The automation toolkit: small tools, permanent wins

Problem

Dozens of repetitive workflows too small to justify a project each: chasing approvals, extracting meeting actions, bulk-editing orders, annotating production statuses. Individually minor. Together, a full-time job.

What I built

A set of small tools that each remove one chore for good. Purchase approvals land in the team's Slack channel the second they are requested, instead of waiting in a portal. After every meeting, AI reads the transcript, pulls out the action items and posts them to the team so nothing gets forgotten. Another tool updates hundreds of orders on its own, clicking through the same screens a person would, in minutes instead of days.

Impact

Core contributors to the 80+ hours of weekly manual work eliminated. Approvals surface in Slack in seconds; meeting actions stopped getting lost; hundred-order edits run in minutes, unattended.

CS/06

Drop production sheets: raw export in, print-floor ready out

Problem

Every limited-edition merch drop arrived as a raw thousand-row export. Turning it into usable production paperwork for three branches took hours of copy-paste and bred exactly the kind of errors a print floor can't afford.

What I built

A tool that turns the raw export into ready-to-use production sheets for each country in one step. Orders come out grouped by design with the biggest print runs first, every design links straight to its artwork, and the team ticks off each item as it is printed and dispatched.

Impact

Sheet preparation went from hours to minutes per drop, and print teams in three countries work from an identical, current document instead of three diverging copies.

CS/07
StrategyRegulatory analysis

EU Customs Reform: a two-page answer to a sixty-page question

Problem

The 2026 EU Customs Reform introduces new duties, thresholds and data requirements. For a fulfilment model spanning the UK, an EU hub in Germany and worldwide customers, the question was blunt: does this break us, and what has to change?

What I built

A two-page executive brief built from the source material itself: the EU regulation, customs rules and carrier guidance. It mapped the 2026 to 2028 timeline against each of our order flows, showed exactly where we were exposed and where we were not, and laid out a short, prioritized to-do list.

Impact

Clarity instead of alarm. The analysis showed the EU-hub structure shields the core flows, isolated the genuinely affected areas, and gave leadership a short action list instead of a consultancy engagement.

CS/08
StrategyMarket research

Brooklyn hiring playbook: finding printmakers where they actually are

Problem

A new Brooklyn production studio needed experienced fine-art printmakers. LinkedIn ads reached almost none of them, because working artists don't job-hunt there for freelance studio work.

What I built

Research across 227 candidate channels, distilled into a phased go-to-market: art-school career boards, niche arts job boards, makerspaces and print studios, trade communities and local classifieds. Free hyper-local tiers first, paid channels only where they earn it, with ad copy guidance and NYC pay-transparency compliance built in.

Impact

A ready-to-execute channel plan that redirected hiring effort from a channel that wasn't working to the places the actual talent pool congregates.
